\author[1]{Xuan Bao}
\author[1]{Yueqin Hu}\cormark[1]

\begin{abstract}
The same hostile sentence may be interpreted differently when its target is marked as female or male. This study asks: do people and large language models (LLMs) give the same aggressiveness rating to the same attack when only the target's gender marking changes? This question is difficult to answer in natural online data, where target gender is usually intertwined with wording, topic, and platform context. We therefore constructed a Chinese minimal gender-reversal corpus of 371 matched attack pairs. Each pair kept the hostile wording and attack domain fixed while changing only the gendered expression marking the target. In a parallel rating task, 779 Chinese participants and nine LLMs judged perceived aggressiveness using 3-point, 7-point, and 0--100 slider scales. We estimated female-minus-male gaps (F-M gaps), located their distribution across attack domains, and compared LLMs with overall-human, female-participant, and male-participant references at aggregate, domain, and item levels. Human raters and LLMs did not give equal ratings: female-targeted versions were rated as more aggressive than matched male-targeted versions. The gap was largest in sexualization and appearance attacks. Model--human similarity changed with the human reference group, response scale, and level of comparison; no model was interchangeable with humans at the item level. These findings indicate that LLMs capture broad human patterns in Chinese gendered hate-speech perception, while alignment claims depend on the human reference, measurement scale, and comparison level.
\end{abstract}

\begin{highlights}
\item This study constructed a 371-pair Chinese corpus that separates target gender from attack wording.
\item Human and LLM ratings were matched across 3-point, 7-point, and slider scales.
\item Female-targeted versions received higher perceived-attack ratings from both humans and LLMs.
\item Sexualization and appearance showed the largest F-M gaps.
\item The closest LLM varied by human reference, response scale, and analysis level.
\end{highlights}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Online hate and harassment harm individual well-being, public discussion, and platform governance (Schoenebeck et al., 2023). Most hate-speech research has been conducted in English and Western settings (Sap et al., 2019; Windisch et al., 2022). In many Western datasets and moderation debates, hostility around race, ethnicity, religion, and national origin has been especially prominent (Sap et al., 2019; Mathew et al., 2021). Chinese online hate requires separate evidence. Chinese platforms use different vocabulary, social references, and interaction norms (Jiang et al., 2022; Jing-Schmidt \& Peng, 2018; Liao, 2024; Lu et al., 2023). The salient hostile themes in Chinese social media are not identical to those in Western settings: gender antagonism, misogynistic expression, and anti-feminist discourse are visible forms of hostility (Jiang et al., 2022; Jing-Schmidt \& Peng, 2018; Liao, 2024). Chinese offensive-language resources have grown in recent years (Deng et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2023). Research on Chinese gendered hate speech remains limited. Existing evidence gives limited guidance on how Chinese users perceive gendered attacks as aggressive or harmful.

Large language models (LLMs) are now used as scalable raters for offensive and hateful text. They are used in content moderation, toxicity scoring, social-bias evaluation, and computational social-science annotation (He et al., 2024; Ziems et al., 2024). Prior work does not support a single conclusion about LLM--human alignment in harmful-language rating. LLMs can sometimes approximate human hate-speech judgements or use context in human-like ways (Davidson, 2026; He et al., 2024; Ziems et al., 2024). Yet LLM judgements also vary across models, prompts, instructions, wording, and rater groups (Chiu et al., 2022; Giorgi et al., 2025; Orlikowski et al., 2025; Sun et al., 2025). The alignment question is therefore not whether LLM ratings are generally reliable. It is when, and relative to which human reference, they converge with human perceptions (Davani et al., 2022; Giorgi et al., 2025; Sun et al., 2025). Hate-speech ratings are social-perceptual judgements. A label such as aggressive, offensive, or discriminatory reflects how a text is experienced within a language community (Cowan \& Hodge, 1996; Davani et al., 2022; Larimore et al., 2021; Sap et al., 2022). In this setting, model ratings should be evaluated against clearly specified human perceptual references (Santy et al., 2023; Orlikowski et al., 2025). In Chinese gendered hate speech, it remains unclear when LLMs converge with human perceptions and when they diverge under comparable materials and rating conditions (Jiang et al., 2022; Deng et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2023).

Testing this alignment requires an appropriate corpus of Chinese gendered attacks. Natural hate-speech corpora are useful because they show how hostility appears online. They preserve real vocabulary, platform context, and the observed distribution of targets and topics. For the present question, however, such corpora raise three problems. First, many widely used resources are English datasets. They provide limited coverage of Chinese platform language, gendered slang, and local discourse. Second, natural collections usually do not balance target gender. Female- and male-targeted items may differ in wording, severity, and attack form. Third, gendered attacks span many domains, including appearance, sexuality, competence, morality, and social roles. Ratings may therefore vary with attack domain as well as target gender. If these factors remain entangled, a higher rating for one target group could reflect the attacked person's gender, stronger wording, a different attack domain, or a different topic distribution (Mathew et al., 2021).

Minimal-pair and counterfactual designs are commonly used in NLP bias research to hold sentence content stable while changing one focal social cue, such as a gendered pronoun, occupation-linked gender cue, or gender inflection (Lu et al., 2018; Rudinger et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2018; Zmigrod et al., 2019). This controlled comparison helps reduce alternative explanations based on wording, topic, or sentence content. Similar controlled test-case logic has also been used to diagnose hate-speech models (R\"ottger et al., 2021).

The present study applies this logic to Chinese gendered attack sentences. The focal contrast is whether the same attack wording refers to a female or male target. The resulting corpus balances female- and male-targeted versions while covering multiple attack domains. This design estimates the female-minus-male target-gender gap (F-M gap), defined as the rating for the female-targeted version minus the rating for the matched male-targeted version, while holding wording and attack force constant. The domain labels matter because the same gender contrast can have different meanings in sexualization, appearance, capability, morality, or social-role attacks. Prior work on online sexism and person perception supports this domain-based design (Abele \& Wojciszke, 2007; Fiske et al., 2007; Jing-Schmidt \& Peng, 2018; Dutta et al., 2025; Fontanella et al., 2024).

Human--LLM alignment also depends on which humans define the reference. A pooled human average is a useful benchmark. It can also hide subgroup differences that matter for gendered hate-speech perception. Female and male raters may perceive the same gender-targeted attack differently. Their ratings may reflect different social positions, exposure histories, or personal relevance. Prior annotation research treats such disagreement as meaningful variation in perception (Davani et al., 2022; Larimore et al., 2021). Prior model-comparison work also shows that model outputs can align more closely with some annotator groups than with others (Santy et al., 2023; H. Sun et al., 2025; Orlikowski et al., 2025). In gendered hate-speech evaluation, female and male human subgroups may therefore need to be examined separately, because an aggregate reference can conceal different perceptual standards for the same hostile text.

Perceived aggressiveness is also measured through a response scale. A scale defines how much distinction a rater can express. A 3-point scale asks for coarse categories. A 7-point scale and a 0--100 slider allow more graded judgements. More response options can increase discrimination. They can also increase dispersion or inconsistent use of the numerical space. Human psychometric research shows that scale format can affect reliability, validity, discrimination, and response distributions (Preston \& Colman, 2000; Lozano et al., 2008; Simms et al., 2019; Abulela \& Khalaf, 2024; Kloft et al., 2024; Haslbeck et al., 2025; Vollbracht et al., 2026). The same issue is less studied for LLMs. A response-scale change may simply rescale model output. It may also change the model's rating pattern and its alignment with human ratings. Scale format is therefore a measurement condition for human--LLM alignment.

This study asks three research questions.

\textbf{RQ1.} When attack content is held constant, are female-targeted and matched male-targeted versions rated as equally aggressive by humans and LLMs?

\textbf{RQ2.} Does the F-M gap differ across attack domains, and do humans and LLMs show similar domain patterns?

\textbf{RQ3.} How do response scale and human reference group change which LLM appears closest to human ratings across overall effect size, domain pattern, and item-level correspondence?

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Human raters}
Target gender refers to whether the attacked person in a sentence is female or male. Participant gender refers to the rater's reported gender.

Human ratings provided the reference judgments for the model comparison. Chinese participants were recruited online through social media. After quality-control screening, the analytic sample contained 779 valid participants.

Each valid participant was randomly assigned to one response format. The rating task included approximately 75 templates covering all 10 first-level attack domains, randomized item order, and attention checks. Human participants saw only one target-gender version of each sampled template. The human sample size was planned to yield roughly 25 ratings for each independent sentence version within each response format. In the realized analytic sample, $75 \times 779 / (3 \text{ response formats} \times 2 \text{ target-gender versions} \times 371 \text{ templates}) \approx 26.2$ ratings were obtained per sentence version, where the female- and male-targeted versions of the same template were treated as separate sentence versions.

During recruitment and assignment, participant counts were monitored to keep the six scale-by-participant-gender cells (three response formats $\times$ two participant-gender groups) approximately balanced (Table~\ref{tab:session-balance}). The participant sample was concentrated among 18--34-year-old Chinese internet users (Table~\ref{tab:participant-demographics}). Item-level means were computed separately for all participants, female participants, and male participants. The all-participant estimate is referred to as the overall-human reference. These three human references were compared with the nine LLMs. All experiments received host-institution ethics approval.

\subsection{LLM raters}
LLM ratings provided parallel automated judgments under the same item and scale structure. Nine contemporary LLMs were queried in November 2025 through the OpenRouter API using an OpenAI-compatible client: Anthropic Claude-Opus-4.5, DeepSeek-R1, DeepSeek-V3.2, Google Gemma-4-31B, Meta Llama-4-Maverick, Moonshot Kimi-K2-Thinking, OpenAI GPT-5.1, Qwen-2.5-72B-Instruct, and Z-AI GLM-4.6. The panel was selected to cover widely used closed- and open-weight model families available through the same inference interface.

Each model rated both versions of every minimal pair under all three response formats. The prompt specified the rating construct and the response scale, and each model returned a structured numeric rating for each item. The main minimal-pair analyses use zero-shot ratings. Temperature was set to 0. The analysis treats each production model as a fixed rater, so population-level variation across LLMs is left unestimated.

\subsection{Materials: minimal gender-reversal corpus}
The corpus was designed to isolate target gender from attack content. A native Chinese-speaking research team constructed 371 minimal pairs of attack-laden sentences, yielding 742 gender-targeted sentence versions. Within each pair, the two versions were identical except for a gender-referent substitution (e.g., \emph{she} $\leftrightarrow$ \emph{he}; \emph{woman} $\leftrightarrow$ \emph{man}; female-coded vs.\ male-coded names or kin terms). This construction holds wording, attack content, and pragmatic force constant while varying the target's gender referent.

The item pool used a three-level coding framework. The framework drew on prior work on interpersonal derogation, gendered hostility, online sexism, and person evaluation, plus inductive review of Chinese online hostile expressions (Cowan \& Hodge, 1996; Abele \& Wojciszke, 2007; Fiske et al., 2007; Jing-Schmidt \& Peng, 2018; Jiang et al., 2022; Dutta et al., 2025; Fontanella et al., 2024). The first level contained 10 broad attack domains: sexualization, appearance, gender role/expression, interpersonal relations, moral character, economic resources, social status, emotional stability, capability/competence, and intellectual rationality. Within each domain, second- and third-level labels captured more specific attack attributes and strategies observed in Chinese hostile expressions. These finer labels guided stimulus construction and are shown in Appendix Table~\ref{tab:stimulus-examples} for representative examples. Domain labels were used to test whether the F-M gap was concentrated in specific forms of attack.

Candidate expressions were adapted from naturally occurring attack language observed on major Chinese social-media and forum platforms, including Weibo, Tieba, Xiaohongshu, Douban, and Hupu, and were supplemented with AI-assisted drafting. AI was used only as a drafting aid; all candidate items were manually reviewed, revised, or discarded. Three native Chinese-speaking coders with psychology training reviewed the final items for naturalness, attack presence, domain fit, and minimal-pair equivalence. Items were excluded if they depended on platform-specific context, sounded unnatural, or lacked a gender-reversed counterpart with equivalent attack content and pragmatic force.

\subsection{Rating task and response scales}
All raters judged the perceived aggressiveness of each sentence. Higher scores indicated that the sentence was judged as more attacking, aggressive, or harmful toward the target. Human and LLM raters used the same three response formats. The \textbf{3-point category} scale was labelled $0 =$ non-attacking, $1 =$ mildly attacking, and $2 =$ strongly attacking. The \textbf{7-point Likert-type} scale was labelled $0 =$ non-attacking, $1 =$ very mildly attacking, $2 =$ mildly attacking, $3 =$ moderately attacking, $4 =$ clearly attacking, $5 =$ strongly attacking, and $6 =$ extremely attacking. The \textbf{0--100 slider} was anchored at $0 =$ non-attacking and $100 =$ extremely attacking. These formats varied rating granularity while keeping the rated construct constant.

Human and LLM procedures used the same item set and scale definitions. Human participants completed the task online after random assignment to one response scale. The assignment ensured that a participant did not rate both target-gender versions of the same template. Item order was randomized. Sentence versions were paired by template after aggregation.

LLM ratings followed the same item and scale structure. For each model, both gender versions of every template were rated under each response format. The prompt specified the rating scale and requested a structured numeric response; ratings were then parsed and paired by template, target gender, model, and scale before analysis.

\subsection{Statistical analysis}
The paired item was the central unit of analysis. The main item-level quantity was the F-M gap in perceived attack ratings, defined as the mean rating for the female-targeted version minus the mean rating for the matched male-targeted version:
\[
  \Delta^{(i)}_{F-M} \;=\; s^{(i)}_F - s^{(i)}_M ,
\]
where $s^{(i)}_F$ and $s^{(i)}_M$ are the mean perceived attack ratings of the female-targeted and male-targeted versions of item $i$ within a rater group and scale. The summary effect size for a given rater group and scale was Cohen's $d_z$,
\[
  d_z(r, c) \;=\; \frac{\overline{\Delta^{(i)}_{F-M}}_i}{\widehat{\mathrm{SD}}\!\left(\Delta^{(i)}_{F-M}\right)_i},
\]
computed across the 371 paired items as a paired standardized effect size (Cohen, 1988). A positive $\Delta_{F-M}$ means that the female-targeted version was rated as more aggressive than the matched male-targeted version. The full design crossed 12 rater groups (nine LLMs plus three human references: overall-human, female-participant, and male-participant references) with three scales, yielding 36 rater-by-scale combinations. For descriptive cross-scale comparisons, item means and F-M gaps were normalized by scale maximum to place the three scales on a common 0--1 axis; $d_z$ itself is scale-free.

The analyses had two purposes. First, we tested the F-M gap. Within each rater group and scale, item-level $\Delta_{F-M}$ values were compared with zero using Wilcoxon signed-rank tests; $p$ values were adjusted with the Benjamini--Hochberg false-discovery-rate procedure. We report mean $\Delta_{F-M}$ and $d_z$. Intervals for mean F-M gaps and model--human differences used paired-item percentile bootstrap resampling over the 371 templates. The same computation was repeated within each first-level attack domain to locate where the F-M gap was concentrated. Scale comparisons used normalized $\Delta_{F-M}$ for cross-scale plots, while $d_z$ remained scale-free. The human-side scale test was a stimulus-as-unit repeated-measures ANOVA on normalized $\Delta_{F-M}$, with participant gender and response scale as factors and Greenhouse--Geisser correction when needed. To test whether response format affected humans and LLMs differently, a second stimulus-as-unit repeated-measures ANOVA compared the overall-human reference with the nine-model LLM panel mean, using rater type and response scale as within-item factors. This model treats the nine LLMs as the fixed model panel evaluated in this study. For the $d_z$ trajectory, paired-item bootstrap resampling compared scale-related $d_z$ changes for the overall-human reference and the LLM panel mean.

Second, the closeness analysis compared each LLM with human ratings under fixed conditions. Model ratings were compared with each human reference group and response scale. Aggregate closeness was the absolute model--human difference in overall $d_z$. Domain-profile closeness was Spearman's $\rho$ between model and human vectors of domain-specific F-M gaps across the 10 first-level domains. Because this correlation used only 10 domain ranks, large correlations can arise by chance and standard large-sample approximations are not ideal. Permutation tests are useful in such settings because they compare the observed statistic with a reference distribution generated under random rearrangements rather than relying only on large-sample approximations (Good, 2005; Ernst, 2004). The two-sided permutation test therefore used random domain rankings as the chance baseline, with $10^5$ random rank permutations. Item-level correspondence was ICC(2,1) across the 371 paired-item $\Delta_{F-M}$ values.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Female-targeted versions receive higher perceived-aggressiveness ratings}
\label{sec:result-rq1a}

Minimal-pair and counterfactual designs are commonly used to isolate a focal social cue while holding sentence content stable (Lu et al., 2018; Rudinger et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2018; Zmigrod et al., 2019). This logic is also consistent with behavioral and functional testing in NLP, where constructed test cases are used to diagnose specific model behaviors rather than average accuracy alone (Ribeiro et al., 2020; R\"ottger et al., 2021). The present corpus applied this logic to perceived aggressiveness. Each attack template had matched female- and male-targeted versions with the same hostile wording. This pairing allowed a direct test of whether target-gender marking changed perceived aggressiveness. For each rater-by-scale cell, item-level $\Delta_{F-M}$ values were compared with zero.

Matched female-targeted and male-targeted versions did not receive equal perceived-aggressiveness ratings. Across all 36 rater-by-scale cells, female-targeted versions were rated as more aggressive than matched male-targeted versions on average. Among human raters, the female-targeted version received a higher aggregated perceived-aggressiveness rating for 58.2\%--74.7\% of items, depending on participant group and response scale. Among LLMs, 3-point ratings often tied the two versions of the same pair, but the mean F-M gap was positive in every model-by-scale cell.

Because the direction test was repeated across many LLM model-by-scale comparisons, the LLM $p$ values were adjusted using the Benjamini--Hochberg false-discovery-rate procedure (Benjamini and Hochberg, 1995). This correction makes the positive-direction claim more conservative by controlling the expected false-discovery rate across the 27 LLM tests. All 27 LLM cells reached BH-FDR adjusted $q < 10^{-7}$ for $\Delta_{F-M} > 0$. This result establishes the direction of the F-M gap: when attack content was held constant, female-targeted versions were rated as more aggressive than their matched male-targeted versions.

\subsection{Human reference group changes the magnitude comparison}
\label{sec:result-rq1b}

An overall positive F-M gap does not determine which human reference should be used to evaluate model ratings. The choice of human reference matters because annotation research shows that disagreement in subjective tasks such as aggression, toxicity, and hate-speech judgement can reflect systematic differences in annotator experience, identity, and values rather than random noise (Davani et al., 2022; Larimore et al., 2021; Sap et al., 2022). Recent work on model positionality and subjective judgement further shows that model--human alignment depends on which human group is used as the reference (Santy et al., 2023; Orlikowski et al., 2025; Sun et al., 2025). Accordingly, each LLM was compared with three human references--overall humans, female participants, and male participants--under the same materials and response scales.

Figure~\ref{fig:direction-agreement} summarizes the normalized mean F-M gap for each rater group and response scale. Within human participants, male participants generally showed the smallest F-M gaps, female participants generally showed larger gaps, and the overall-human reference fell between the two subgroup references. The LLM panel did not occupy a stable interval between the male- and female-participant references. Kimi-K2-Thinking, GPT-5.1, and Qwen-2.5-72B exceeded the female-participant mean F-M gap on at least one scale, while other models were closer to the overall-human or male-participant references. Figure~\ref{fig:direction-agreement} should therefore be read as a map of model- and scale-specific magnitudes, not as evidence that LLM values are bounded by the two human subgroups.

  \label{fig:direction-agreement}
\end{figure}

The human reference groups differed in effect size across all response formats. Male participants had $d_z = 0.219$, $0.198$, and $0.167$ on the 3-point, 7-point, and slider scales. Female participants had $d_z = 0.655$, $0.704$, and $0.590$. The overall-human reference had $d_z = 0.621$, $0.588$, and $0.512$. Thus, female and male participants shared the same positive direction, but female participants showed a larger F-M gap. The overall-human reference is the primary benchmark for model comparison because it summarizes all valid participants; the female- and male-participant references show how subgroup differences can change the apparent model--human match.

Using the 3-point scale as an illustration, the nine LLMs had $d_z$ values from 0.289 to 0.653. DeepSeek-R1 and DeepSeek-V3.2 were at the lower end ($d_z = 0.289$), GLM-4.6 was similar ($d_z = 0.297$), and GPT-5.1 was at the upper end ($d_z = 0.653$). These values were all above the male-participant reference and mostly below the overall-human and female-participant references, with GPT-5.1 approaching the female-participant value. The broader conclusion is not that LLMs always fall between female and male participants. Rather, humans and LLMs share the positive direction of the F-M gap, while the magnitude comparison depends on which human reference group and response scale are used.

\subsection{Sexualization and appearance are stable high-gap domains}
\label{sec:result-domain}

The overall F-M gap does not show which kinds of attack content carried the target-gender asymmetry. This question is especially important for Chinese gendered hate speech because prior work shows that Chinese online sexism and toxic language are structured by fine-grained categories, lexical forms, and gendered social meanings rather than by a single uniform form of hostility (Jiang et al., 2022; Jing-Schmidt and Peng, 2018; Lu et al., 2023). Reviews of online sexism and misogyny likewise show that gendered abuse is studied through multiple conceptual and measurement categories, which makes a domain-level analysis more appropriate than treating gendered hate as a single undifferentiated construct (Dutta et al., 2025; Fontanella et al., 2024). The corpus therefore allowed the F-M gap to be localized across 10 attack domains. For each domain and response scale, we computed Cohen's $d_z$ for the paired F-M gap.

The results showed that F-M gaps were not evenly distributed across attack domains. Figure~\ref{fig:domain-alignment} shows domain-level $d_z$ values for the overall-human reference, female participants, male participants, and the LLM median. Each row is one attack domain, so the four rater summaries for the same domain can be compared directly within each response scale.

Sexualization was the most consistent high-gap domain in human ratings. For the overall-human reference, sexualization had $d_z = 1.47$, $1.42$, and $1.18$ on the 3-point, 7-point, and slider scales. Appearance was also a stable high-gap domain, with overall-human $d_z = 0.76$, $0.80$, and $0.61$. Other domains also showed positive F-M gaps, especially on the 3-point scale, so the domain result should not be read as only two domains mattering. The clearer conclusion is that target gender mattered most reliably in domains tied to sexual shaming and bodily or appearance-based evaluation.

The LLM median profile partly matched this domain pattern. On the 7-point and slider scales, appearance and sexualization were the two largest LLM-median domains. On the 3-point scale, the LLM median was less concentrated in those two domains and assigned large effects to several other attack domains. Thus, humans and LLMs shared the broad importance of sexualization and appearance, but the exact domain ordering differed by response scale.

\subsection{Response format changes the F-M gap}
\label{sec:result-scale}

Response format is a measurement condition in perceived-aggressiveness rating. Psychometric work shows that the number and format of response options can change reliability, validity, discrimination, and the use of the response distribution (Preston and Colman, 2000; Lozano et al., 2008; Simms et al., 2019; Haslbeck et al., 2025; Vollbracht et al., 2026). For LLM ratings, the same scale change also changes the instruction space given to the model, so it cannot be assumed to be a neutral rescaling. Because humans and LLMs rated the same 371 pairs under 3-point, 7-point, and slider formats, this design provided a direct test of whether rating granularity changed the measured F-M gap similarly for the two rater types. Scale effects were summarized with scale-free $d_z$ trajectories, and rater-type by response-format differences were tested using normalized $\Delta_{F-M}$.

Figure~\ref{fig:scale-response} visualizes the cross-scale $d_z$ trajectories; Table~\ref{tab:scale-response} reports the exact $d_z$ values and trend labels for the same trajectories. Human effects stayed flat or decreased as the scale became finer: the overall-human reference moved from $0.621$ to $0.588$ to $0.512$, male participants from $0.219$ to $0.198$ to $0.167$, and female participants from $0.655$ to $0.704$ to $0.590$. Most LLMs moved in the opposite direction. Six of nine models showed monotonic or near-monotonic increases, with Gemma-4-31B rising from $0.408$ to $0.773$ to $1.016$.

The rater-type contrast was supported by a stimulus-as-unit repeated-measures ANOVA on normalized $\Delta_{F-M}$ that compared the overall-human reference with the nine-model LLM panel mean. The rater-type by response-format interaction was significant, $F(2, 740) = 6.41$, $p = .002$. Scale effects were significant within the overall-human reference, $F(2, 740) = 10.73$, $p < .001$, and within the LLM panel mean, $F(2, 740) = 63.31$, $p < .001$. The standardized $d_z$ trajectory showed the same endpoint contrast: from the 3-point scale to the slider, the overall-human $d_z$ decreased by $0.109$, whereas the LLM panel mean $d_z$ increased by $0.180$, yielding a bootstrapped interaction in $d_z$ change of $0.289$, 95\% CI $[0.144, 0.454]$, BH-FDR adjusted $q = .0006$. The adjacent 3-point-to-7-point and 7-point-to-slider $d_z$ contrasts were weaker, with adjusted $q = .074$ for both.

This scale dependence changed the human--LLM comparison. On the 3-point scale, five of nine LLMs were below the overall-human $d_z$; on the slider, five of nine were at or above it. This pattern does not mean that a finer response scale always makes a fixed model more human-like. It means that, in the present data, response format changed the measured effect size and therefore changed which models appeared closer to the overall-human reference.

A human-side repeated-measures ANOVA clarified the human component of this pattern. In a stimulus-as-unit analysis of normalized $\Delta_{F-M}$, participant gender had a substantially larger effect, $F(1, 370) = 111.17$, $p < .001$, $\eta^2_G = .044$, than scale, $F(2, 740) = 9.44$, Greenhouse--Geisser corrected $p < .001$, $\eta^2_G = .007$. The participant-gender by scale interaction was not significant, $F(2, 740) = 1.79$, $p = .169$. Female participants exceeded male participants within every scale after BH-FDR correction. Thus, participant gender was the larger human-side determinant of the F-M gap, while scale remained a smaller but detectable measurement condition.

\subsection{Closest human--LLM match depends on reference group and response scale}
\label{sec:result-closeness}

Aggregate model--human similarity can obscure disagreement at finer levels. This distinction is important because prior work treats LLMs as useful scalable annotators in some settings (He et al., 2024; Ziems et al., 2024), but also shows that subjective text perception varies across models, prompts, and human references (Santy et al., 2023; Orlikowski et al., 2025; Sun et al., 2025). Behavioral testing work similarly cautions that average performance can hide behavior-specific failures (Ribeiro et al., 2020; R\"ottger et al., 2021). Accordingly, model--human alignment was evaluated at three levels: aggregate similarity in overall $d_z$, domain-profile similarity across the 10 attack domains, and item-level correspondence across the 371 paired items.

Aggregate similarity was the absolute difference between an LLM's overall $d_z$ and a human reference group's overall $d_z$. Domain-profile similarity was Spearman's $\rho$ between model and human vectors of $d_z$ across the 10 first-level attack domains. Item-level similarity was ICC(2,1) between model and human $\Delta_{F-M}$ values across the 371 sentence pairs.

Figure~\ref{fig:multilevel-similarity} shows why closest-model labels require caution. The closest point in a row can be near several other models, especially for aggregate $d_z$ gaps. Against the overall-human reference, the smallest aggregate $d_z$ gaps were 0.032, 0.010, and 0.002 across the 3-point, 7-point, and slider scales. These small gaps indicate that some models nearly matched the overall-human effect size under a given scale, but they do not imply item-level interchangeability.

At the domain level, the best 10-domain Spearman correlations ranged from 0.56 to 0.85 across the nine human-reference-by-scale cells. The permutation test was used because each domain-profile correlation was based on only 10 domain ranks, so a seemingly large $\rho$ could still occur under random domain ordering. This resampling logic is appropriate for small rank-based comparisons because it evaluates the observed statistic against a task-specific randomization baseline (Good, 2005; Ernst, 2004). In the two-sided permutation null, two random rankings of 10 items had an approximate 95\% interval of $[-0.65, +0.65]$. Seven of the nine best-model cells reached $p<0.05$ in the per-cell permutation test. The two weaker cells were both anchored on the female-participant reference at coarser scales: 3-point $\rho=0.56$, $p=0.10$, and 7-point $\rho=0.64$, $p=0.06$. These $p$ values are unadjusted for selecting the best model from the nine-model panel.

At the item level, the best ICC(2,1) in each cell remained low, ranging from 0.089 to 0.222. Against the overall-human reference, the best item-level ICC rose from 0.089 on the 3-point scale to 0.158 on the 7-point scale and 0.222 on the slider, with Claude-Opus-4.5 closest on the two finer scales. The item-level result shows that aggregate or domain-level closeness did not make any model interchangeable with human ratings for individual sentence pairs.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Main finding: target gender changes perceived aggressiveness}

The central finding is that humans and LLMs showed the same directional asymmetry under strict content control: female-targeted versions were rated as more aggressive than matched male-targeted versions. Because each pair held attack wording constant, this result is not a comparison between two naturally different corpora. It shows that the gender of the attacked target can change the perceived force of the same Chinese attack sentence. The minimal-pair design is therefore not only a bias-testing device; it is the condition that makes the target-gender contrast interpretable.

\subsection{Domain mechanism: sexualization and appearance carry stronger gendered force}

The F-M gap was strongest in sexualization and appearance. These domains are not merely severe attack domains in general; they are domains where Chinese online hostility often draws on gendered expectations about sexual respectability, bodily evaluation, and public appearance. Under this interpretation, the female-targeted version can be perceived as more aggressive because the same wording activates a stronger gendered social meaning. LLMs reproduced the broad concentration of the gap in these domains, suggesting that model ratings can reflect some domain-level regularities in human perception, even though the exact domain ordering was not identical across models, humans, and scales.

\subsection{Human reference: pooled and subgroup comparisons answer different questions}

The overall-human reference is the clearest comparison point for asking whether an LLM resembles the combined human rating pattern in this sample. The female- and male-participant references answer a different question: whether the pooled human average hides subgroup variation. In this study, female participants showed larger F-M gaps than male participants, which means that a model can look close to one human subgroup and farther from another. This does not mean that an LLM should be judged by whether it resembles female or male raters specifically. It means that model--human similarity is partly a function of which human comparison group is chosen.

\subsection{Scale mechanism: response format changes the measured gap}

Response scale affected the measured F-M gap rather than simply translating the same judgement onto a different numerical range. Coarser scales can compress small distinctions and create more ties, whereas finer scales give raters more room to express graded differences. In the present data, humans and LLMs responded to this extra room differently: human effects were flat or smaller on finer scales, whereas most LLM effects increased. This pattern should be read as a measurement result for the current task and model panel, not as a general rule that finer scales make LLMs more human-like.

\subsection{Scope of the claims}

These findings support a controlled conclusion: when Chinese attack content is held constant, humans and LLMs both show a positive F-M gap, and that gap is strongest in sexualization and appearance. The study does not show that the same pattern will hold across all languages, platforms, model versions, or real conversational contexts. The controlled design removes wording and topic confounds, but it also removes reply context, multimodal cues, audience effects, and evolving platform meanings. The alignment results should therefore be read as evidence about this minimal-pair measurement setting, with the broader limitations developed below.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}

Several limitations define the scope of these conclusions. First, the minimal-pair items are Chinese. Whether the same target-gender and scale-response patterns hold in English or other languages remains open, especially because gender marking, pronoun systems, and platform discourse differ across languages. Second, the participant pool was a nonrepresentative Chinese online sample with a young, urban skew. The overall-human reference is the main benchmark in this study, but it should be read as the overall reference for this participant pool, not for all Chinese speakers. Third, the LLM panel is a snapshot of November 2025 production models. Later model versions, task-specific fine-tuning, and different prompting protocols may change the results.

Fourth, the minimal-pair design isolates target gender but necessarily simplifies the pragmatic context of real hate speech. Real online attacks can include reply context, multimodal cues, coded references, and audience-specific meanings. Fifth, participant gender was treated as binary because the available sample supported stable female- and male-participant comparisons; larger samples are needed to examine more nuanced identity variables. Finally, the three response formats differ not only in granularity but also in anchor structure, cognitive demand, and numerical freedom. Future work should isolate these mechanisms more directly.

\section{Conclusion}

When attack content was held fixed, female-targeted versions were rated as more aggressive than matched male-targeted versions by both humans and LLMs. The F-M gap was especially visible in sexualization and appearance, although exact domain ordering varied by rater type and scale. Human--LLM alignment depended on the human reference group, response scale, and analysis level. Overall humans provide the main benchmark, while subgroup references show how pooled averages can hide meaningful variation. Response scale should be treated as a measurement condition, not a prompt detail. No model was interchangeable with human ratings at the item level, even when aggregate effect sizes were close.
