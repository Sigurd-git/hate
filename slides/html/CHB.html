Measurement-Conditional Human--LLM Alignment in Gender-Differential Attack Ratings

Xuan Bao

Yueqin Hu

Abstract

This study tests whether the same Chinese attack is judged more aggressive when the attacked person is female rather than male, and whether large language models (LLMs) reproduce the corresponding human rating pattern. In naturally occurring Chinese online text, gendered hostility is usually confounded with wording, topic, and platform context. To isolate the attacked person’s gender, we constructed 371 minimal pairs of Chinese attack sentences that held hostile content constant while changing whether the target was female or male. The pairs covered 10 attack domains. Perceived aggressiveness was rated by 779 Chinese participants and nine LLMs using 3-point, 7-point, and 0–100 slider scales. We examined whether female- and male-targeted versions received equal ratings, whether the female-minus-male gap (F-M gap; female-targeted rating minus matched male-targeted rating) differed across attack domains for humans and LLMs, and whether model–human alignment changed across human reference groups, response scales, and analysis levels. Both humans and LLMs rated female-targeted versions as more aggressive than matched male-targeted versions. The F-M gap was largest for sexualization and appearance attacks. Female participants showed larger F-M gaps than male participants, so pooled human averages masked subgroup differences. The closest LLM depended on the human comparison group, response scale, and alignment criterion. These results show that LLMs can reproduce the broad direction and attack-domain pattern of human ratings, but model–human similarity is conditional on the human reference and measurement scale used.

Introduction

Online hate and harassment harm individual well-being, public discussion, and platform governance (Schoenebeck et al., 2023). Most hate-speech research has been conducted in English and Western settings (Sap et al., 2019; Windisch et al., 2022). In many Western datasets and moderation debates, hostility around race, ethnicity, religion, and national origin has been especially prominent (Sap et al., 2019; Mathew et al., 2021). Chinese online hate requires separate evidence. Chinese platforms use different vocabulary, social references, and interaction norms (Jiang et al., 2021; Jing-Schmidt & Peng, 2018; Liao, 2024; Lu et al., 2023). The salient hostile themes in Chinese social media are not identical to those in Western settings: gender antagonism, misogynistic expression, and anti-feminist discourse are visible forms of hostility (Jiang et al., 2021; Jing-Schmidt & Peng, 2018; Liao, 2024). Chinese offensive-language resources have grown in recent years (Deng et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2023). Research on Chinese gendered hate speech remains limited.

Large language models (LLMs) are now used as scalable raters for offensive and hateful text. This use raises a measurement problem: judging harmful language is a human perceptual task (Cowan & Hodge, 1996; Davani et al., 2022; Larimore et al., 2021; Sap et al., 2022). The key question is whether LLM ratings converge with the human reference that defines this task.

This study examines whether Chinese human raters and contemporary LLMs perceive matched female-targeted and male-targeted versions of the same attack sentence differently. The focal contrast is the attacked person’s gender marking: whether otherwise matched attack wording refers to a female or male target. The main comparison is the female-minus-male target-gender gap (F-M gap), defined as the rating for the female-targeted version minus the rating for the matched male-targeted version. The study uses a Chinese minimal gender-reversal corpus, human and LLM raters, and three response formats to test whether an F-M gap appears, where it is concentrated by attack domain, and whether human–LLM alignment depends on human reference group, response scale, and analysis level.

Research Background and Measurement Framework

LLM hate-speech ratings require a human perceptual reference

LLMs are increasingly used as scalable raters for offensive and hateful text in content moderation, toxicity scoring, social-bias evaluation, and computational social-science annotation (He et al., 2024; Ziems et al., 2024). Prior work does not support a single conclusion about LLM–human alignment in harmful-language rating. LLMs can sometimes approximate human hate-speech judgements or use context in human-like ways (Davidson, 2026; He et al., 2024; Ziems et al., 2024). Yet LLM judgements also vary across models, prompts, instructions, wording, and rater groups (Chiu et al., 2022; Giorgi et al., 2025; Orlikowski et al., 2025; Sun et al., 2025).

The alignment question is therefore not whether LLM ratings are generally reliable. It is when, and relative to which human reference, they converge with human perceptions (Davani et al., 2022; Giorgi et al., 2025; Sun et al., 2025). Hate-speech ratings are social-perceptual judgements. A label such as aggressive, offensive, or discriminatory reflects how a text is experienced within a language community (Cowan & Hodge, 1996; Davani et al., 2022; Larimore et al., 2021; Sap et al., 2022). In this setting, model ratings should be evaluated against clearly specified human perceptual references (Santy et al., 2023; Orlikowski et al., 2025).

Chinese gendered hate requires controlled comparison

Chinese gendered hate speech requires separate evidence because the social targets and hostile themes of Chinese online interaction are not simply translations of Western hate-speech settings. Chinese platforms use different vocabulary, social references, and interaction norms (Jiang et al., 2021; Jing-Schmidt & Peng, 2018; Liao, 2024; Lu et al., 2023). In Chinese social media, gender antagonism, misogynistic expression, and anti-feminist discourse are visible forms of hostility (Jiang et al., 2021; Jing-Schmidt & Peng, 2018; Liao, 2024). Existing Chinese offensive-language resources have expanded (Deng et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2023), but they still give limited guidance on how Chinese users perceive gendered attacks as aggressive or harmful.

Natural hate-speech corpora are useful because they show how hostility appears online. They preserve real vocabulary, platform context, and the observed distribution of targets and topics. For the present question, however, such corpora raise three problems. First, many widely used resources are English datasets. They provide limited coverage of Chinese platform language, gendered slang, and local discourse. Second, natural collections usually do not balance target gender. Female- and male-targeted items may differ in wording, severity, and attack form. Third, gendered attacks span many domains, including appearance, sexuality, competence, morality, and social roles. Ratings may therefore vary with attack domain as well as target gender. If these factors remain entangled, a higher rating for one target group could reflect the attacked person’s gender, stronger wording, a different attack domain, or a different topic distribution (Mathew et al., 2021).

Minimal gender-reversal design and attack domains

Minimal-pair and counterfactual designs are commonly used in NLP bias research to hold sentence content stable while changing one focal social cue, such as a gendered pronoun, occupation-linked gender cue, or gender inflection (Lu et al., 2018; Rudinger et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2018; Zmigrod et al., 2019). This controlled comparison helps reduce alternative explanations based on wording, topic, or sentence content. Similar controlled test-case logic has also been used to diagnose hate-speech models (Röttger et al., 2021).

The present study applies this logic to Chinese gendered attack sentences. The focal contrast is whether the same attack wording refers to a female or male target. The resulting corpus balances female- and male-targeted versions while covering multiple attack domains. This design estimates the F-M gap while holding wording and attack force constant.

Attack domain is also part of the measurement framework. The same gender contrast can have different meanings in sexualization, appearance, capability, morality, or social-role attacks. Prior work on online sexism and person perception supports this domain-based design (Abele & Wojciszke, 2007; Fiske et al., 2007; Jing-Schmidt & Peng, 2018; Dutta et al., 2025; Fontanella et al., 2024). The present study uses attack-domain labels to locate where the F-M gap is concentrated.

Human subgroups in gendered hate perception

Human–LLM alignment also depends on which humans define the reference. A pooled human average is a useful benchmark. It can also hide subgroup differences that matter for gendered hate-speech perception. Female and male raters may perceive the same gender-targeted attack differently. Their ratings may reflect different social positions, exposure histories, or personal relevance. Prior annotation research treats such disagreement as meaningful variation in perception (Davani et al., 2022; Larimore et al., 2021). Prior model-comparison work also shows that model outputs can align more closely with some annotator groups than with others (Santy et al., 2023; H. Sun et al., 2025; Orlikowski et al., 2025). In gendered hate-speech evaluation, female and male human subgroups may therefore need to be examined separately, because an aggregate reference can conceal different perceptual standards for the same hostile text.

Response scales as measurement conditions

Perceived aggressiveness is measured through a response scale. A scale defines how much distinction a rater can express. A 3-point scale asks for coarse categories. A 7-point scale and a 0–100 slider allow more graded judgements. More response options can increase discrimination. They can also increase dispersion or inconsistent use of the numerical space. Human psychometric research shows that scale format can affect reliability, validity, discrimination, and response distributions (Preston & Colman, 2000; Lozano et al., 2008; Simms et al., 2019; Abulela & Khalaf, 2024; Kloft et al., 2024; Haslbeck et al., 2025; Vollbracht et al., 2026). The same issue is less studied for LLMs. A response-scale change may simply rescale model output. It may also change the model’s rating pattern and its alignment with human ratings. Scale format is therefore a measurement condition for human–LLM alignment.

The Present Study

The present study tests Chinese gendered hate perception with 371 minimal pairs of attack-laden sentences. Each pair differs only in the target’s gender referent. Human raters and nine LLMs rated the same materials under 3-point category, 7-point Likert, and 0–100 slider formats. The primary outcome is the paired F-M gap in perceived attack ratings, ΔF−M\Delta_{F-M}, summarized with Cohen’s dzd_z across paired items. The study asks three research questions.

RQ1. When attack content is held constant, are female-targeted and matched male-targeted versions rated as equally aggressive by humans and LLMs?

RQ2. Does the F-M gap differ across attack domains, and do humans and LLMs show similar domain patterns?

RQ3. How do response scale and human reference group change which LLM appears closest to human ratings across overall effect size, domain pattern, and item-level correspondence?

Methods

Human raters

Target gender refers to whether the attacked person in a sentence is female or male. Participant gender refers to the rater’s reported gender.

Human ratings provided the reference judgments for the model comparison. Chinese participants were recruited online through social media. After quality-control screening, the analytic sample contained 779 valid participants.

Each valid participant was randomly assigned to one response format. The rating task included approximately 75 templates covering all 10 first-level attack domains, randomized item order, and attention checks. Human participants saw only one target-gender version of each sampled template. The human sample size was planned to yield roughly 25 ratings for each independent sentence version within each response format. In the realized analytic sample, 75×779/(3 response formats×2 target-gender versions×371 templates)≈26.275 \times 779 / (3 \text{ response formats} \times 2 \text{ target-gender versions} \times 371 \text{ templates}) \approx 26.2 ratings were obtained per sentence version, where the female- and male-targeted versions of the same template were treated as separate sentence versions.

During recruitment and assignment, participant counts were monitored to keep the six scale-by-participant-gender cells (three response formats ×\times two participant-gender groups) approximately balanced (Table 1). The participant sample was concentrated among 18–34-year-old Chinese internet users (Table 2). Item-level means were computed separately for all participants, female participants, and male participants. The all-participant estimate is referred to as the overall-human reference. These three human references were compared with the nine LLMs. All experiments received host-institution ethics approval.

LLM raters

LLM ratings provided parallel automated judgments under the same item and scale structure. Nine contemporary LLMs were queried in November 2025 through the OpenRouter API using an OpenAI-compatible client: Anthropic Claude-Opus-4.5, DeepSeek-R1, DeepSeek-V3.2, Google Gemma-4-31B, Meta Llama-4-Maverick, Moonshot Kimi-K2-Thinking, OpenAI GPT-5.1, Qwen-2.5-72B-Instruct, and Z-AI GLM-4.6. The panel was selected to cover widely used closed- and open-weight model families available through the same inference interface.

Each model rated both versions of every minimal pair under all three response formats. The prompt specified the rating construct and the response scale, and each model returned a structured numeric rating for each item. The main minimal-pair analyses use zero-shot ratings. Temperature was set to 0. The analysis treats each production model as a fixed rater, so population-level variation across LLMs is left unestimated.

Materials: minimal gender-reversal corpus

The corpus was designed to isolate target gender from attack content. A native Chinese-speaking research team constructed 371 minimal pairs of attack-laden sentences, yielding 742 gender-targeted sentence versions. Within each pair, the two versions were identical except for a gender-referent substitution (e.g., she ↔\leftrightarrow he; woman ↔\leftrightarrow man; female-coded vs. male-coded names or kin terms). This construction holds wording, attack content, and pragmatic force constant while varying the target’s gender referent.

The item pool used a three-level coding framework. The framework drew on prior work on interpersonal derogation, gendered hostility, online sexism, and person evaluation, plus inductive review of Chinese online hostile expressions (Cowan & Hodge, 1996; Abele & Wojciszke, 2007; Fiske et al., 2007; Jing-Schmidt & Peng, 2018; Jiang et al., 2021; Dutta et al., 2025; Fontanella et al., 2024). The first level contained 10 broad attack domains: sexualization, appearance, gender role/expression, interpersonal relations, moral character, economic resources, social status, emotional stability, capability/competence, and intellectual rationality. Within each domain, second- and third-level labels captured more specific attack attributes and strategies observed in Chinese hostile expressions. These finer labels guided stimulus construction and are shown in Appendix Table 5 for representative examples. Domain labels were used to test whether the F-M gap was concentrated in specific forms of attack.

Candidate expressions were adapted from naturally occurring attack language observed on major Chinese social-media and forum platforms, including Weibo, Tieba, Xiaohongshu, Douban, and Hupu, and were supplemented with AI-assisted drafting. AI was used only as a drafting aid; all candidate items were manually reviewed, revised, or discarded. Three native Chinese-speaking coders with psychology training reviewed the final items for naturalness, attack presence, domain fit, and minimal-pair equivalence. Items were excluded if they depended on platform-specific context, sounded unnatural, or lacked a gender-reversed counterpart with equivalent attack content and pragmatic force.

Rating task and response scales

All raters judged the perceived aggressiveness of each sentence. Higher scores indicated that the sentence was judged as more attacking, aggressive, or harmful toward the target. Human and LLM raters used the same three response formats: a 3-point category scale (0=0 = non-attacking, 1=1 = mildly attacking, 2=2 = strongly attacking), a 7-point Likert-type scale from 0=0 = non-attacking to 6=6 = strongly attacking, and a 0–100 slider from 0=0 = non-attacking to 100=100 = strongly attacking. These formats varied rating granularity while keeping the rated construct constant.

Procedure

Human and LLM procedures used the same item set and scale definitions. Human participants completed the task online after random assignment to one response scale. The assignment ensured that a participant did not rate both target-gender versions of the same template. Item order was randomized. Sentence versions were paired by template after aggregation.

LLM ratings followed the same item and scale structure. For each model, both gender versions of every template were rated under each response format. The prompt specified the rating scale and requested a structured numeric response; ratings were then parsed and paired by template, target gender, model, and scale before analysis.

Statistical analysis

The paired item was the central unit of analysis. The main item-level quantity was the F-M gap in perceived attack ratings, defined as the mean rating for the female-targeted version minus the mean rating for the matched male-targeted version: ΔF−M(i)=sF(i)−sM(i),\Delta^{(i)}_{F-M} \;=\; s^{(i)}_F - s^{(i)}_M , where sF(i)s^{(i)}_F and sM(i)s^{(i)}_M are the mean perceived attack ratings of the female-targeted and male-targeted versions of item ii within a rater group and scale. The summary effect size for a given rater group and scale was Cohen’s dzd_z, dz(r,c)=ΔF−M(i)¯iSD̂(ΔF−M(i))i,d_z(r, c) \;=\; \frac{\overline{\Delta^{(i)}_{F-M}}_i}{\widehat{\mathrm{SD}}\!\left(\Delta^{(i)}_{F-M}\right)_i}, computed across the 371 paired items as a paired standardized effect size (Cohen, 1988). A positive ΔF−M\Delta_{F-M} means that the female-targeted version was rated as more aggressive than the matched male-targeted version. The full design crossed 12 rater groups (nine LLMs plus three human references: overall-human, female-participant, and male-participant references) with three scales, yielding 36 rater-by-scale combinations. For descriptive cross-scale comparisons, item means and F-M gaps were normalized by scale maximum to place the three scales on a common 0–1 axis; dzd_z itself is scale-free.

The analyses had two purposes. First, we tested the F-M gap. Within each rater group and scale, item-level ΔF−M\Delta_{F-M} values were compared with zero using Wilcoxon signed-rank tests; pp values were adjusted with the Benjamini–Hochberg false-discovery-rate procedure. We report mean ΔF−M\Delta_{F-M} and dzd_z. Intervals for mean F-M gaps and model–human differences used paired-item percentile bootstrap resampling over the 371 templates. The same computation was repeated within each first-level attack domain to locate where the F-M gap was concentrated. Scale comparisons used normalized ΔF−M\Delta_{F-M} for cross-scale plots, while dzd_z remained scale-free. The human-side scale test was a stimulus-as-unit repeated-measures ANOVA on normalized ΔF−M\Delta_{F-M}, with participant gender and response scale as factors and Greenhouse–Geisser correction when needed.

Second, we asked how close each LLM was to human ratings under fixed conditions. Model ratings were compared with each human reference group and response scale. Aggregate closeness was the absolute model–human difference in overall dzd_z. Domain-profile closeness was Spearman’s ρ\rho between model and human vectors of domain-specific F-M gaps across the 10 first-level domains. The two-sided permutation pp value used chance domain rankings as the baseline, with 10510^5 random rank permutations. Item-level correspondence was ICC(2,1) across the 371 paired-item ΔF−M\Delta_{F-M} values.

Results

The Results are organized in five steps. First, they establish the direction of the F-M gap. Second, they show how the size of that gap changes with the human reference group. Third, they locate the gap across attack domains. Fourth, they examine response scale as a measurement condition. Fifth, they compare human–LLM similarity across aggregate, domain, and item levels.

Female-targeted versions receive higher perceived-aggressiveness ratings

Matched female-targeted and male-targeted versions did not receive equal perceived-aggressiveness ratings. Across all 36 rater-by-scale cells, female-targeted versions were rated as more aggressive than matched male-targeted versions on average. Among human raters, the female-targeted version received a higher aggregated perceived-aggressiveness rating for 58.2%–74.7% of items, depending on participant group and response scale. Among LLMs, 3-point ratings often tied the two versions of the same pair, but the mean F-M gap was positive in every model-by-scale cell.

Because the direction test was repeated across many LLM model-by-scale comparisons, the LLM pp values were adjusted using the Benjamini–Hochberg false-discovery-rate procedure. This correction makes the positive-direction claim more conservative by controlling the expected false-discovery rate across the 27 LLM tests. All 27 LLM cells reached BH-FDR adjusted q<10−7q < 10^{-7} for ΔF−M>0\Delta_{F-M} > 0. This result establishes the direction of the F-M gap: when attack content was held constant, female-targeted versions were rated as more aggressive than their matched male-targeted versions.

Human reference group changes the magnitude comparison

Figure 2 summarizes the F-M gap for each rater group and response scale. Within human participants, male participants generally showed the smallest F-M gaps, female participants generally showed larger gaps, and the overall-human reference fell between the two subgroup references. LLMs often fell between the male- and female-participant references, but this was not a rule: some model-by-scale combinations approached or exceeded the female-participant reference, especially under the finer response formats.

The human reference groups differed in effect size across all response formats. Male participants had dz=0.219d_z = 0.219, 0.1980.198, and 0.1670.167 on the 3-point, 7-point, and slider scales. Female participants had dz=0.655d_z = 0.655, 0.7040.704, and 0.5900.590. The overall-human reference had dz=0.621d_z = 0.621, 0.5880.588, and 0.5120.512. Thus, female and male participants shared the same positive direction, but female participants showed a larger F-M gap. The overall-human reference is the primary benchmark for model comparison because it summarizes all valid participants; the female- and male-participant references show how subgroup differences can change the apparent model–human match.

Using the 3-point scale as an illustration, the nine LLMs had dzd_z values from 0.289 to 0.653. DeepSeek-R1 and DeepSeek-V3.2 were at the lower end (dz=0.289d_z = 0.289), GLM-4.6 was similar (dz=0.297d_z = 0.297), and GPT-5.1 was at the upper end (dz=0.653d_z = 0.653). These values were all above the male-participant reference and mostly below the overall-human and female-participant references, with GPT-5.1 approaching the female-participant value. The broader conclusion is not that LLMs always fall between female and male participants. Rather, humans and LLMs share the positive direction of the F-M gap, while the magnitude comparison depends on which human reference group and response scale are used.

Sexualization and appearance are stable high-gap domains

The domain-level analysis showed that F-M gaps were not evenly distributed across attack domains. Figure 3 shows domain-level dzd_z values for the overall-human reference, female participants, male participants, and the LLM median. Each row is one attack domain, so the four rater summaries for the same domain can be compared directly within each response scale.

Sexualization was the most consistent high-gap domain in human ratings. For the overall-human reference, sexualization had dz=1.47d_z = 1.47, 1.421.42, and 1.181.18 on the 3-point, 7-point, and slider scales. Appearance was also a stable high-gap domain, with overall-human dz=0.76d_z = 0.76, 0.800.80, and 0.610.61. Other domains also showed positive F-M gaps, especially on the 3-point scale, so the domain result should not be read as only two domains mattering. The clearer conclusion is that target gender mattered most reliably in domains tied to sexual shaming and bodily or appearance-based evaluation.

The LLM median profile partly matched this domain pattern. On the 7-point and slider scales, appearance and sexualization were the two largest LLM-median domains. On the 3-point scale, the LLM median was less concentrated in those two domains and assigned large effects to several other attack domains. Thus, humans and LLMs shared the broad importance of sexualization and appearance, but the exact domain ordering differed by response scale.

Response format changes the F-M gap

The response-scale analysis showed a rater-type contrast in how rating granularity changed the measured F-M gap. Figure 4 and Table 3 report the cross-scale trajectory of dzd_z. Human effects stayed flat or decreased as the scale became finer: the overall-human reference moved from 0.6210.621 to 0.5880.588 to 0.5120.512, male participants from 0.2190.219 to 0.1980.198 to 0.1670.167, and female participants from 0.6550.655 to 0.7040.704 to 0.5900.590. Most LLMs moved in the opposite direction. Six of nine models showed monotonic or near-monotonic increases, with Gemma-4-31B rising from 0.4080.408 to 0.7730.773 to 1.0161.016.

This pattern does not mean that a finer response scale always makes a fixed model more human-like. It means that, in the present data, response format changed the measured effect size and therefore changed which models appeared closer to the overall-human reference.

Closest human–LLM match depends on reference group and response scale

The final analysis examined whether aggregate similarity implied finer-grained interchangeability. Three levels of similarity were separated. Aggregate similarity was the absolute difference between an LLM’s overall dzd_z and a human reference group’s overall dzd_z. Domain-profile similarity was Spearman’s ρ\rho between model and human vectors of dzd_z across the 10 first-level attack domains. Item-level similarity was ICC(2,1) between model and human ΔF−M\Delta_{F-M} values across the 371 sentence pairs.

Figure 5 shows why closest-model labels require caution. The closest point in a row can be near several other models, especially for aggregate dzd_z gaps. Against the overall-human reference, the smallest aggregate dzd_z gaps were 0.032, 0.010, and 0.002 across the 3-point, 7-point, and slider scales. These small gaps indicate that some models nearly matched the overall-human effect size under a given scale, but they do not imply item-level interchangeability.

At the domain level, the best 10-domain Spearman correlations ranged from 0.56 to 0.85 across the nine human-reference-by-scale cells. Because there were only 10 domains, rank correlations had a wide chance band: a two-sided permutation null with two random rankings of 10 items had an approximate 95% interval of [−0.65,+0.65][-0.65, +0.65]. Discussion

Main finding: target gender changes perceived aggressiveness

The central finding is that humans and LLMs showed the same directional asymmetry under strict content control: female-targeted versions were rated as more aggressive than matched male-targeted versions. Because each pair held attack wording constant, this result is not a comparison between two naturally different corpora. It shows that the gender of the attacked target can change the perceived force of the same Chinese attack sentence. The minimal-pair design is therefore not only a bias-testing device; it is the condition that makes the target-gender contrast interpretable.

Domain mechanism: sexualization and appearance carry stronger gendered force

The F-M gap was strongest in sexualization and appearance. These domains are not merely severe attack domains in general; they are domains where Chinese online hostility often draws on gendered expectations about sexual respectability, bodily evaluation, and public appearance. Under this interpretation, the female-targeted version can be perceived as more aggressive because the same wording activates a stronger gendered social meaning. LLMs reproduced the broad concentration of the gap in these domains, suggesting that model ratings can reflect some domain-level regularities in human perception, even though the exact domain ordering was not identical across models, humans, and scales.

Human reference: pooled and subgroup comparisons answer different questions

The overall-human reference is the clearest comparison point for asking whether an LLM resembles the combined human rating pattern in this sample. The female- and male-participant references answer a different question: whether the pooled human average hides subgroup variation. In this study, female participants showed larger F-M gaps than male participants, which means that a model can look close to one human subgroup and farther from another. This does not mean that an LLM should be judged by whether it resembles female or male raters specifically. It means that model–human similarity is partly a function of which human comparison group is chosen.

Scale mechanism: response format changes the measured gap

Response scale affected the measured F-M gap rather than simply translating the same judgement onto a different numerical range. Coarser scales can compress small distinctions and create more ties, whereas finer scales give raters more room to express graded differences. In the present data, humans and LLMs responded to this extra room differently: human effects were flat or smaller on finer scales, whereas most LLM effects increased. This pattern should be read as a measurement result for the current task and model panel, not as a general rule that finer scales make LLMs more human-like.

Scope of the claims

These findings support a controlled conclusion: when Chinese attack content is held constant, humans and LLMs both show a positive F-M gap, and that gap is strongest in sexualization and appearance. The study does not show that the same pattern will hold across all languages, platforms, model versions, or real conversational contexts. The controlled design removes wording and topic confounds, but it also removes reply context, multimodal cues, audience effects, and evolving platform meanings. The alignment results should therefore be read as evidence about this minimal-pair measurement setting, with the broader limitations developed below.

Limitations and Future Work

Several limitations define the scope of these conclusions. First, the minimal-pair items are Chinese. Whether the same target-gender and scale-response patterns hold in English or other languages remains open, especially because gender marking, pronoun systems, and platform discourse differ across languages. Second, the participant pool was a nonrepresentative Chinese online sample with a young, urban skew. The overall-human reference is the main benchmark in this study, but it should be read as the overall reference for this participant pool, not for all Chinese speakers. Third, the LLM panel is a snapshot of November 2025 production models. Later model versions, task-specific fine-tuning, and different prompting protocols may change the results.

Fourth, the minimal-pair design isolates target gender but necessarily simplifies the pragmatic context of real hate speech. Real online attacks can include reply context, multimodal cues, coded references, and audience-specific meanings. Fifth, participant gender was treated as binary because the available sample supported stable female- and male-participant comparisons; larger samples are needed to examine more nuanced identity variables. Finally, the three response formats differ not only in granularity but also in anchor structure, cognitive demand, and numerical freedom. Future work should isolate these mechanisms more directly.

Conclusion

When attack content was held fixed, female-targeted versions were rated as more aggressive than matched male-targeted versions by both humans and LLMs. The F-M gap was especially visible in sexualization and appearance, although exact domain ordering varied by rater type and scale. Human–LLM alignment depended on the human reference group, response scale, and analysis level. Overall humans provide the main benchmark, while subgroup references show how pooled averages can hide meaningful variation. Response scale should be treated as a measurement condition, not a prompt detail. No model was interchangeable with human ratings at the item level, even when aggregate effect sizes were close.
